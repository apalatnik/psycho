Preregistration Report

About the Final Project of the Experimental Psychology Lab

Background

The Goal of our study is to replicate and extend the original study “Give me Gestalt! Preference for Cubist Artwork Revealing High Detectability of Objects” by Claudia Muth and several other members of the Department of General Psychology and Methodology at the University of Bamberg submitted on 21 November 2012. This project is part of our Experimental Psychology Lab Course at University Osnabrück. The theoretical background lies mainly in the Gestalt theory. Scientists have hypothesized that perceptual grouping processes are linked with the ‘reward system’, neural structures that induce a pleasant sensation after finishing a task. This study is the first to show empirically that in fact there is a connection between detectability of Gestalt or objects in challenging visual stimuli and a pleasing sensation. This is a highly interesting observation as it is in conflict with statements such as that viewers derive aesthetic pleasure from violations of their perceptual predictions.

Research Questions

[H1] Do participants without expertise in cubism appreciate cubist artworks more if they can easily detect concealed objects in them?

[H2] Does synthetic cubism show higher detectability than analytical cubism?

[H3] Do participants without expertise in cubism appreciate colorful cubist artworks more than black & white cubist artworks?

Design of the Experiment

General remarks about the design

        

Sampling plan

        We plan to recruit twenty participants. The participant pool should consist of an approximately equal amount of male and female participants. Participants will be recruited either in person or via messenger applications as soon as the preregistration is finished and the implementation of the experiment is finalized. We will exclude children, people with cubist expertise, color blindness, no english knowledge or neurological disorders.

Materials

        We use Ishihara palettes found on the internet and cubist images which were given to us by other replicators of the same study. The cubist images are the same as used in the original study as they were originally provided by the conductors of the original study. All materials which are used for our experiment can be found in this repository: https://github.com/apalatnik/psycho

Procedure

        First, the participant has to undergo some Ishihara tests. The study is structured in two blocks, each showing the stimuli in a randomized order. During the first block, subjects have to rate the pictures on liking. During the second block, participants rate how well they could detect objects within the artwork. Each block consists of the same 60 pictures. All ratings are chosen from a 7-point-Likert-scale from 1 (‘not at all’) to 7 (‘very’). Before the actual trials the participant has to go through a few test trials in order to minimize misunderstandings. The trial structure is kept simple. The participants are presented a picture and below the picture there is a question similar to “How much do you like this picture?” or “How well is the detectability of objects in this picture?” and they have to click a button (buttons from 1 to 7)  in order to answer it.

Measured variables

colour vision :

  •  measured by the performance in Ishihara tests
  • either impaired or non-impaired colour vision

liking-score :

  • measured by rating scale data from the first block
  • ordinal variable

detectability-score :

  • measured by rating scale data from the second block
  • ordinal variable

response time :

  • measured by the time from arrival of the stimulus until rating
  • ratio level variable

Analysis Plan

Exclusion criteria

For single trial data:

  • reaction time is too quick or too long

For data from a whole participant:

  • mean reaction time is far below or above average
  • bad performance on the Ishihara tests
  • same answers for all pictures

Confirmatory hypothesis testing

We plan to cast the data into appropriate types in order to be able to run a cumulative model regressing liking-score on detectability-score. This is done by using the ‘brms’ package. Furthermore, using the ‘brms’ package we will analyse the mean detectability-score for synthetic and analytical cubist artwork as well as the liking-score for colorful and non-colorful cubist artwork and check for their significance. We will formulate the research questions into hypotheses now:

        [H1] Participants without expertise in cubism appreciate cubist artworks more if they can easily detect concealed objects in them.

[H2] Synthetic cubism shows higher detectability than analytical cubism.

[H3] Participants without expertise in cubism appreciate colorful cubist artworks more than monochrome cubist artworks.

If [H1] is true, we expect a significant positive gradient in the regression line and the gradient being above 0 with a probability of 95%. If [H2] is true, we expect the mean detectability-scores to be significantly greater for synthetic cubism than for analytical cubism. If [H3] is true, we expect the mean liking-scores to be significantly greater for colorful cubist artworks than for non-colorful cubist artworks. For [H3] we will also check the detectability-score of the pictures because the detectability might be greater for colorful artwork than non-colorful artwork.
